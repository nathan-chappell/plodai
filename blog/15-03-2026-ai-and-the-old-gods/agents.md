# AI and the Old Gods: Working Brief

## Purpose

This file replaces the earlier conversation dump and acts as the standing brief for drafting the article.

## Core idea

The article should connect three threads:

1. Neural networks are more than "fancy autocomplete"; they are mathematically rich computational systems.
2. Humans have always reacted to opaque power with mythic language.
3. The most important question may not be whether AI is conscious, but what long-term interaction with persuasive symbolic systems does to us.

## Target outcome

Write a LinkedIn article that is:

- intellectually serious
- readable to technical and adjacent readers
- reflective rather than alarmist
- strong enough to feel original without becoming grandiose

## Audience

Primary audience:

- software engineers
- technical founders
- product people
- thoughtful generalist readers on LinkedIn

Assume readers are AI-curious, but not specialists in dynamical systems or philosophy of mind.

## Tone

- clear
- essayistic
- confident
- restrained
- thoughtful rather than sensational

Avoid:

- breathless doom framing
- mystical endorsement of AI
- overly academic prose
- sounding like a thread turned into paragraphs

## Structure to preserve

The current draft should roughly move in this order:

1. Why scale neural networks at all?
2. Neural networks as dynamical systems.
3. Arbitrary computation and opacity.
4. Why humans mythologize opaque power.
5. Why AI interaction may shape users.
6. Why symbolic fluency is not the same as experience.
7. A closing warning about reorganizing ourselves around systems we do not understand.

## Claims to handle carefully

- Recurrent neural networks and arbitrary computation:
  keep technically accurate, but explain plainly.
- Chaos / fractals / strange attractors:
  use as illumination, not decoration.
- Historical comparison to gods or demons:
  frame as a pattern in human explanation, not as a cheap dunk on religion.
- Behavioral effects of AI:
  present as a serious concern or hypothesis, not as settled science unless sourced.

## Writing guidance

- Lead with curiosity, not conclusion.
- Prefer strong transitions between sections.
- Use one concrete example when a section becomes abstract.
- Keep paragraphs fairly tight for LinkedIn readability.
- Save the sharpest sentence for the closing paragraph.

## Open questions for later drafting

- Which technical references should be named directly versus implied?
- How explicit should the article be about current LLMs versus neural networks in general?
- Should the tone land more philosophical or more practical in the conclusion?
- Do we want one short personal anecdote near the middle to keep it grounded?

## Possible sources or anchors to revisit

- early neural computation work from the late 1980s and early 1990s
- Pollack and related recurrent network work
- Turing completeness of recurrent neural networks
- work on language, cognition, and behavioral adaptation
