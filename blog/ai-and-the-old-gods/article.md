# AI and the Old Gods

## Working thesis

Modern AI feels uncanny not because it is supernatural, but because we are once again confronting a powerful system whose inner workings are largely opaque to human intuition. Neural networks are not gods, and they are not minds in any ordinary embodied sense, but they may still alter the people who use them every day.

## Outline

### 1. Start with the investigation

- Open with the motivating question: why are companies spending billions to scale neural networks?
- Frame the inquiry as a technical and philosophical investigation into what kind of thing a neural network actually is.
- Introduce the central answer: neural networks are attractive because they can act as highly general computational systems.
- Transition into the idea that understanding them requires looking at them as dynamical systems, not just as pattern-matching software.

### 2. Neural networks as dynamical systems

- Explain that a neural network evolves through state space, especially in the recurrent case.
- Introduce the older research tradition that studied neural networks through nonlinear dynamics rather than today’s product language.
- Bring in examples like strange attractors, fractal structure, and sensitivity to initial conditions.
- Emphasize the shift in perspective: these systems are not merely statistical tools, but mathematically rich systems with behavior that can become hard to interpret.
- Transition to the next question: if they are that rich, how computationally powerful are they?

### 3. Arbitrary computation and what it implies

- Introduce Turing completeness in plain language.
- Explain the result that recurrent neural networks can, under certain conditions, simulate arbitrary computation.
- Use the `0^n1^n` example as a compact illustration of how continuous states can encode symbolic structure.
- Draw out the consequence: once a system can implement arbitrary computation, predicting its behavior becomes deeply difficult.
- Connect this to computational irreducibility and the limits of outside prediction.
- Transition from technical power to the human experience of confronting systems that are both powerful and opaque.

### 4. The old pattern: opacity invites mythology

- Shift from computer science to history and anthropology.
- Note that humans have long responded to opaque but consequential forces with mythic explanations.
- Use examples like storms, illness, and fate to show the recurring pattern.
- Make the key distinction carefully: ancient explanations often got the mechanism wrong, but they were responding to something real, namely power without transparency.
- Relate that pattern to modern reactions to AI, where people often slip into mystical language around systems they do not understand.
- Transition to why this matters even if the machine itself is not conscious.

### 5. AI may change us even if it does not understand

- Argue that the deeper concern is not whether the system has a soul, but what repeated interaction does to the human user.
- Bring in the point that language use and conversational habits shape cognition and behavior.
- Include the politeness example: saying "please" to a model may matter less for the model than for the habits it trains in us.
- Explore the possibility that always-available conversational systems create reinforcement loops that can shape attention, dependence, and judgment.
- Transition to a clarifying section on what these systems are and are not.

### 6. Symbol manipulation without lived experience

- Clarify the formalist view: these models manipulate symbols and learned representations.
- Distinguish fluent language generation from grounded understanding.
- Emphasize that the model has no direct sensory contact with the world and no lived perspective.
- Explain why this gap matters: outputs can feel deeply meaningful even when they arise from statistical structure rather than experience.
- Transition to the closing warning: a persuasive symbolic system can still become socially powerful.

### 7. Closing warning

- Bring the threads together: neural networks are mathematically powerful, operationally opaque, and socially intimate.
- Reject both naive dismissal and mystical exaggeration.
- Argue that the real risk is not that we have summoned gods, but that we may reorganize ourselves around systems we barely understand.
- End on a memorable line that returns to the title: the old gods may be gone, but the human tendency to submit to mystery is not.

## Notes for expansion

- Keep the voice essayistic, not academic.
- Use technical ideas as support, not as the whole article.
- Let each section earn the next one; the piece works best if it feels like a discovery, not a lecture.
